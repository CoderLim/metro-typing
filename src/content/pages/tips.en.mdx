## Accuracy beats raw speed

In Metro Typing, a typo does not just look bad on the scoreboard — the train **stops** until the station name is correct. That pause often costs more than the time you hoped to save by rushing.

Aim for clean station names first. Speed usually follows once the sticky stretches stop breaking your rhythm. If you only chase the result numbers, you tend to fail at the same stop again on the next round.

## Warm up on a familiar short stretch

Start with a line you already ride, or a short section with fewer stations. Familiar order lowers memory load so you can notice **typing** problems instead of guessing every next stop.

When the short stretch feels automatic, move to a longer route. Long lines such as Seoul Line 1 or the Line 2 loop are better as later challenges than as a first warm-up.

## Preview the next station

A common community habit: while you finish the current name, quietly recall the **next** stop. When your hands lead and your memory lags, typos rise. When the word is ready before your fingers move, accuracy often steadies.

Saying the next station out loud (or under your breath) is enough. You do not need a complicated system — just one station of look-ahead.

## Drill sticky stations alone

Most players do not fail evenly. They have one transfer cluster, one similar-sounding pair, or one long spelling that keeps breaking the run.

After a finish, write down (or remember) those stops. Replay the same line and slow down only on that stretch. Two or three short sessions a week on the **same line and direction** smooth early sticky sections faster than switching routes every day.

## Keep Hangul input clean

Autocomplete and the wrong keyboard language can insert characters you did not intend. Before a practice set:

- Confirm the input language is Hangul when you want Korean station names
- Disable aggressive autocomplete if it keeps “helping” with the wrong word
- Prefer short, deliberate bursts over panic mashing when a name looks unfamiliar

If you are practicing English romanization mode instead, keep that choice consistent for the whole session so your finger paths do not fight two spellings at once.

## Compare like with like

Finish time only means something when the route is fixed. A short line with long names is not the same challenge as a long line with short names. Keep **line + direction** constant when you track personal bests.
