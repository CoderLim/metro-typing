Metro Typing works because station names are familiar words with a fixed order. You are not typing random syllables. You are recalling the next stop, checking the spelling, and moving your hands through a real sequence.

## Start with one line

Pick a line you already know and stay with one direction. Familiar order lowers the memory load, so you can notice typing problems instead of guessing the next station every few seconds.

If you miss the same station twice, slow down for that name on the next run. Metro Typing rewards clean rhythm more than raw speed. A single typo stops the train and breaks the whole run.

## Watch the transition points

Transfer stations and similarly named stops are where many players hesitate. Say the next station quietly before typing it. That short pause often prevents the kind of typo that costs more time than the pause itself.

## Review right after finishing

The result screen gives you time, speed, and accuracy. Treat those numbers as a map of the route. A better record usually comes from one sticky stretch getting smoother, not from forcing every key faster.

Two or three short sessions on the same line can make the station order feel automatic. Once that happens, move to a longer line or switch direction.
