Metro Typing is not only a keyboard challenge. It also tests how well you know the order of stations on a subway line.

## Pick a Line You Actually Know

Start with a route you ride often. Familiar stations make it easier to predict the next answer, and that keeps your typing rhythm from breaking. Once your record stabilizes, try a less familiar line.

## Direction Changes Everything

The same line feels different when the direction changes. If you always commute one way, the reverse order can feel surprisingly hard. Practicing both directions is a simple way to find gaps in your mental map.

## Watch Transfer Stations

Transfer stations often slow players down because they are easy to remember as landmarks but harder to place in exact order. If your run stalls near a transfer, replay the nearby section and focus on the two or three stations before and after it.

## Review the Result Screen

After each run, look beyond the final time. Accuracy and repeated stops tell you where memory and typing are not aligned yet. A slightly slower but cleaner run is often a better foundation for a new record.

Station order gives Metro Typing its shape. Learn the line, keep your hands steady, and the run starts to feel less like guessing and more like driving the route from memory.
